# Staffing Vertical — Objection Handling

## Overview

Staffing objections are predictable. Engineering leaders and TA teams have heard from hundreds of staffing firms and developed reflexive responses. The key is acknowledging the objection as valid, then reframing with specifics.

Never argue. Never dismiss. Always pivot to the specific value CirrusLabs delivers for the specific role they need filled.

---

## Objection 1: "We handle recruiting internally"

**What they mean:** "Our TA team or I handle hiring, and I don't think we need outside help."

**Why they say it:** Habit, cost sensitivity, or genuine confidence in their recruiting process.

**Response framework:**
- Acknowledge that internal recruiting works well for many roles
- Narrow to the specific hard-to-fill roles where internal recruiting struggles
- Offer a low-commitment way to test the relationship

**Example response:**
> That makes sense — internal recruiting is usually the best channel for most roles. Where we tend to help is with the specialized positions that take 2-3x longer to fill: data engineers with Spark/Airflow experience, ML engineers with production deployment backgrounds, cloud architects who've done large-scale migrations. If any of those are sitting open longer than you'd like, I can send over a few anonymized profiles just to see if there's a fit. No commitment, no pressure.

**Key principle:** Position as a supplement for niche roles, not a replacement for their recruiting function.

## Objection 2: "We already have a staffing vendor"

**What they mean:** "We have an existing relationship and switching costs."

**Why they say it:** Inertia, contractual obligations, or genuine satisfaction with the existing vendor.

**Response framework:**
- Do not trash the existing vendor — that backfires
- Ask about specific gaps the current vendor does not cover
- Offer to fill a specific role type as a trial alongside the existing vendor

**Example response:**
> Totally understand — most companies we work with have existing staffing relationships. We're not trying to replace that. What we hear most often is that generalist vendors do well for frontend and full-stack roles but struggle with the more specialized data/ML/cloud positions. If that sounds familiar, it might be worth trying us on one of those harder-to-fill roles to see if we can complement what you already have. Happy to share a couple of candidate profiles for [specific open role] so you can compare quality.

**Key principle:** Coexist, do not displace. Win on quality for the niche roles the existing vendor cannot fill.

## Objection 3: "Contractors are too expensive"

**What they mean:** "The bill rate is higher than an FTE salary, and I can't justify the cost."

**Why they say it:** They are comparing hourly bill rates to FTE base salary without accounting for total cost of employment.

**Response framework:**
- Acknowledge the rate comparison concern
- Reframe to total cost: FTE all-in cost (salary + benefits + equity + recruiting fees + onboarding + risk of bad hire) vs. contract cost
- Highlight the cost of the vacancy (delayed roadmap, team burnout, lost revenue)
- Emphasize flexibility: no severance, no long-term commitment, scale down when the project ends

**Example response:**
> I hear that a lot. On the surface, a $140/hour bill rate looks expensive compared to a $180K salary. But when you add benefits, equity, recruiting fees (typically 20-25% of salary for FTE), and the 3-4 months of ramp time, an FTE costs $250-300K in the first year — and if the hire doesn't work out in 6 months, you're back to zero. A contractor at $140/hour for 6 months is about $145K with zero long-term obligation and zero severance risk. The real cost question is: what does every week of having that role empty cost your roadmap?

**Key principle:** Reframe from rate comparison to total cost of outcome. The vacancy is the most expensive option.

## Objection 4: "We need FTEs, not contractors"

**What they mean:** "We want permanent team members, not temporary staff."

**Why they say it:** Genuine preference for team stability, or belief that contractors are less committed.

**Response framework:**
- Validate the preference for permanence
- Introduce contract-to-hire as a risk-reduction path to FTE
- Highlight that many contractors convert to FTE after 3-6 months
- Frame the contract period as an extended working interview

**Example response:**
> Completely understand — building a permanent team is the goal. That's actually why contract-to-hire is our most popular model. The engineer works embedded in your team for 3-6 months on a contract basis. If they're a great fit — and they usually are — you convert them to FTE with full confidence. It's basically a 3-month working interview instead of a 3-hour panel interview. You see how they code, how they collaborate, how they handle your actual problems. The conversion rate on our placements is significantly higher than cold FTE hires for exactly that reason.

**Key principle:** Contract-to-hire is the bridge. It reduces the risk of a bad FTE hire, which is far more expensive than a contract markup.

## Objection 5: "We tried staffing before and it didn't work"

**What they mean:** "Previous staffing vendors sent underqualified candidates, and I wasted time interviewing them."

**Why they say it:** This is the most common and most valid objection. Most staffing experiences are bad.

**Response framework:**
- Acknowledge the bad experience directly — do not minimize it
- Ask what specifically went wrong (candidate quality, speed, communication)
- Explain how CirrusLabs is different with specifics, not vague claims
- Offer a zero-risk trial (send profiles, no obligation to interview)

**Example response:**
> I appreciate you being direct about that. Honestly, most staffing experiences are mediocre — high volume, low quality, and a lot of wasted interview time. Can I ask what specifically didn't work? Was it candidate quality, the screening process, or something else?

> [After they respond with specifics:]

> That's exactly what we hear most. The difference with us is [specific counter to their issue]. For example, if the problem was candidate quality: every engineer we submit has completed a live technical assessment — architecture design, code review, and system design — calibrated to the specific role. We reject about 70% of candidates before they ever reach you. I'd rather send you 2 strong profiles than 10 mediocre ones. Want me to send a couple of profiles for [open role] so you can judge the quality yourself? No commitment — just profiles to review.

**Key principle:** Validate the bad experience, get specifics, counter with how CirrusLabs addresses that exact failure mode. Then prove it with a zero-risk action.

## Objection 6: "We're not hiring right now"

**What they mean:** Either genuinely not hiring, or politely declining.

**Why they say it:** Could be true (budget freeze, recent layoffs, hiring pause) or could be a soft no.

**Response framework:**
- If contradicted by observable signals (they have open roles posted), gently reference it
- If genuinely not hiring, ask when they expect to start and offer to follow up then
- Plant a seed for future contact

**Example response (if they have open roles):**
> Got it — though I did notice a few engineering roles posted on [job board/careers page]. If those are on hold, totally understand. If they're active and you're having trouble finding candidates, I'm happy to share some profiles. Either way, no pressure.

**Example response (if genuinely not hiring):**
> Makes sense. When you do start hiring again — especially for specialized roles like data engineers or cloud architects — we'd love to be on your radar. Mind if I follow up in [timeframe]? In the meantime, I'll keep you on our radar for relevant market insights.

**Key principle:** Respect the timing but leave the door open. Most staffing relationships start 2-3 touches later.

## Objection 7: "Your rate is too high"

**What they mean:** "The bill rate exceeds our budget or approved rate range."

**Response framework:**
- Ask what their target rate range is
- Explain the rate composition (candidate pay + CirrusLabs margin + benefits/admin)
- Offer to adjust scope (seniority level, contract duration, part-time) to fit budget
- Reframe to value: what does a faster fill save them?

**Example response:**
> Appreciate the transparency. What rate range works for your budget? Our rates reflect the seniority and specialization of the candidates — a senior data engineer with 5+ years of Spark production experience commands a different rate than a mid-level generalist. If the budget is firm, we can look at adjusting seniority level, or we can discuss a longer engagement term which sometimes allows us to be more flexible on rate. What matters most to you — the rate itself or the total cost of getting this role filled quickly?

**Key principle:** Never drop rate without adjusting scope. Rate flexibility should come from engagement structure, not margin erosion.

---

## Objection 8: "Just send me candidates and we'll talk"

**What they mean:** "I don't want a sales conversation. Show me what you have."

**Why they say it:** They are tired of vendor calls and want to evaluate quality before investing time.

**Response framework:**
- This is actually a buying signal disguised as a brush-off
- Comply immediately — send 2-3 anonymized profiles within 24-48 hours
- Include a brief note with each profile explaining fit
- Follow up 3-5 days later

**Example response:**
> Happy to. I'll send over 2-3 anonymized profiles that match your [role] requirements by [tomorrow/end of day]. Each will include a brief note on why they're a fit. If any look strong, we can set up interviews. If not, no hard feelings.

**Key principle:** When they ask to see candidates, give them candidates. Speed and quality of the profiles IS the sales pitch. Do not insist on a meeting first.

---

## General Objection Handling Rules

1. **Never argue.** Acknowledge first, always.
2. **Get specific.** Generic rebuttals fail. Understand their exact concern.
3. **Offer proof, not promises.** Send profiles, share metrics, reference similar situations.
4. **Keep the door open.** Not every objection means no. Many mean "not yet" or "prove it."
5. **Match energy.** If they are brief, be brief. If they engage in detail, go deeper.
6. **Always end with a concrete next step.** Even if the next step is "I'll follow up in Q3."
