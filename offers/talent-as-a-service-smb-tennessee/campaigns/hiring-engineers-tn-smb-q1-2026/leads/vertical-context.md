# Staffing Vertical — Ideal Customer Profile

## Primary Tier: Mid-Market Tech Companies

The ideal CirrusLabs staffing client is a mid-market technology company that has outgrown its internal recruiting capacity for specialized engineering roles. They have the budget, the technical leadership to manage contractors, and the urgency that makes speed worth paying a premium for.

## Firmographics

### Company Size
- **Sweet spot:** 100-2000 employees
- **Engineering team:** 20-300 engineers (large enough to need augmentation, small enough that each hire matters)
- **Why this range:** Below 100 employees, companies rarely have the budget or management layer for contract engineers. Above 2000, they typically have mature internal recruiting and preferred vendor lists that are hard to displace.

### Funding and Revenue
- **VC-backed:** Series B or later (Series A companies are often too early and cost-sensitive)
- **Bootstrapped/public:** $20M+ annual revenue
- **Recent funding round in last 18 months is a strong signal** — capital deployed often means headcount growth

### Industry Segments
- SaaS and platform companies (core tech)
- Fintech and payments
- E-commerce and marketplace platforms
- Data infrastructure and analytics companies
- AI/ML product companies
- Healthcare technology
- Cybersecurity

### Geography
- **Primary:** United States (all major tech hubs)
- **Secondary:** Singapore, UK, Western Europe
- Clients must operate in timezones where CirrusLabs candidates can overlap at least 4 hours

## Technographics

### Stack Alignment (High Priority)
Strong fit when the company's engineering stack overlaps with CirrusLabs candidate specializations:
- **Data engineering:** Spark, Airflow, dbt, Snowflake, Databricks, Kafka, Flink
- **ML/AI:** Python, PyTorch, TensorFlow, MLflow, Kubeflow, feature stores
- **Cloud infrastructure:** AWS, GCP, Azure, Kubernetes, Terraform, Pulumi
- **Backend:** Python, Go, Java, Rust, Node.js (TypeScript)

### Stack Signals (Observable)
- Job posts mentioning specific technologies in CirrusLabs specialization areas
- GitHub org activity in relevant repositories
- Engineering blog posts about scaling data pipelines, ML systems, or cloud migrations

## Behavioral Signals

### Strong Buy Signals
- **Active hiring for roles CirrusLabs specializes in** — multiple open reqs for data engineers, ML engineers, or cloud architects
- **Hiring velocity increase** — 3+ new engineering job posts in the last 30 days
- **Extended time-to-fill** — roles open for 60+ days (visible on job boards)
- **Funding announcement in last 6 months** — capital often translates to headcount
- **Product launch or expansion announced** — creates project-driven hiring urgency
- **Contractor or temp roles posted** — already open to non-FTE models

### Moderate Buy Signals
- Engineering leadership changes (new VP Eng or CTO often reshapes team)
- Office expansion or new location opening
- Technology migration announcements (cloud migration, data platform rebuild)
- Acquiring another company (integration work requires temporary capacity)

### Weak but Relevant Signals
- Conference sponsorships or speaking at data/ML events (indicates investment in the space)
- Job posts for engineering managers (growing team needs more management, which means more ICs coming)
- Glassdoor reviews mentioning understaffing or overwork

## Disqualifiers

Skip companies that match these patterns — they waste pipeline time:

- **Fewer than 50 employees** — rarely have budget or management capacity for contract engineers
- **No engineering presence** — if the company outsources all engineering, they are a delivery client, not staffing
- **Government or defense contractors** — clearance requirements and procurement cycles make staffing impractical
- **Active layoffs** — if they cut engineering headcount in the last 6 months, they are not buying
- **Offshore-first engineering** — companies with 80%+ offshore engineering teams are optimizing for cost, not the speed/quality CirrusLabs sells
- **Existing preferred vendor agreement with lock-in** — unless there is a visible gap the existing vendor cannot fill
- **No open engineering roles for 90+ days** — if they are not hiring, they are not buying staffing
- **Staffing agencies, recruiting firms, headhunters, or talent agencies** — these are direct competitors, not clients; targeting them wastes outreach and harms brand
- **Series Seed or Pre-Seed only** — funding stage too early; budget usually not approved for contract staffing spend
- **Non-tech companies with no engineering team** — if the company has no observable engineering function, they have no need for engineering staffing

## Org Structure Patterns That Indicate Need

### High-Fit Org Patterns
- Dedicated VP of Engineering or CTO with headcount budget authority
- Engineering managers who own hiring for their teams (distributed decision-making)
- Talent Acquisition team that partners with engineering but does not have deep technical sourcing capability
- Teams organized around product areas or domains (data platform team, ML team, infra team) — these create targeted staffing needs

### Low-Fit Org Patterns
- All hiring flows through a centralized procurement or vendor management office (slow, RFP-driven)
- Engineering leadership delegates all hiring to HR with no technical involvement (misaligned decision criteria)
- Flat org with no clear team structure (unclear where a contractor would land)

## ICP Scoring Summary

A company scoring 170+ points across the weighted scoring dimensions (see scoring.md) qualifies for active outreach. Companies scoring 120-169 can be nurtured. Below 120, do not pursue.

The strongest staffing leads combine three elements:
1. They are actively hiring for roles CirrusLabs fills
2. They have the budget and management structure to engage contractors
3. They have urgency — something is driving a timeline (funding, product launch, attrition backfill)

# Staffing Vertical — Buyer Personas

## Overview

Staffing decisions in mid-market tech companies involve 2-4 people. The decision process is faster than consulting or delivery sales — typically 1-3 weeks from first conversation to signed SOW — but the buyer landscape varies by company size and org structure.

Understanding who initiates, who influences, and who signs is critical for targeting the right person with the right message.

---

## Persona 1: VP of Engineering / Head of Engineering

### Role in Buying Process
**Primary decision-maker.** Owns headcount budget, approves new vendor relationships, and has final say on staffing engagements. In companies with 100-500 employees, this person often manages staffing vendors directly.

### Pain Points
- Cannot ship product roadmap on time because the team is understaffed
- Internal recruiting is too slow for specialized roles (data engineering, ML, cloud)
- Losing candidates to competitors who move faster in the hiring process
- Spending personal time interviewing unqualified candidates from generalist agencies
- Board or CEO pressure to deliver against milestones while team is under-resourced

### Priorities
- Speed: How fast can you get qualified candidates in front of me?
- Quality: Will I be interviewing real engineers or resume-padded profiles?
- Low management overhead: Will the contractor be productive without hand-holding?
- Flexibility: Can I scale up for a project push and scale down after?

### Decision Criteria
1. Evidence that the staffing firm understands the technical domain (not just keyword matching)
2. Track record with similar companies and similar roles
3. Speed of first candidate submission (benchmark: under 7 business days)
4. Pricing that fits within approved headcount budget (typically $80-180/hr for senior engineering roles)
5. Low-friction engagement model (no complex procurement, simple MSA + SOW)

### How to Reach
- Email is primary (responsive to concise, signal-specific outreach)
- LinkedIn DM is secondary (respond to relevant, non-generic messages)
- Conferences and meetups (less scalable but high-trust)
- Referrals from other VPs of Engineering (strongest channel)

### Messaging Notes
- Lead with the specific role and signal ("saw you're hiring data engineers")
- Avoid recruiter-speak ("top talent", "best candidates", "full lifecycle")
- Show technical credibility (mention specific technologies, not just role titles)
- Keep it short — they have 200+ unread emails

---

## Persona 2: CTO / Co-founder (Technical)

### Role in Buying Process
**Executive sponsor or approver.** In smaller companies (100-300 employees), the CTO may directly manage staffing. In larger companies, they approve vendor relationships and set hiring strategy but delegate execution to VPs or Eng Managers.

### Pain Points
- Strategic hiring gaps blocking company-level objectives (data platform, AI features, cloud migration)
- Cannot find senior/staff-level engineers through any channel
- Technical debt accumulating because the team is in permanent firefighting mode
- Investors asking about execution velocity and team scaling

### Priorities
- Strategic alignment: Can this vendor fill the exact niche we need, not just generic engineering?
- Candidate caliber: Are these people who could eventually convert to FTE?
- Vendor reliability: Will they deliver consistently, or is this a one-hit operation?
- Cost efficiency: How does contract staffing compare to the all-in cost of a failed FTE hire?

### Decision Criteria
1. Domain expertise in the specific technical area (data/ML/cloud, not generalist)
2. Understanding of the company's technical context and challenges
3. Willingness to discuss contract-to-hire as a path
4. References from peer CTOs or VPs

### How to Reach
- Email (must be highly specific and concise — CTOs delete generic outreach instantly)
- LinkedIn (only if the message demonstrates genuine technical understanding)
- Warm introductions (highest conversion by far)
- CTOs rarely respond to cold calls

### Messaging Notes
- Frame staffing as a strategic tool, not a tactical fix
- Reference the company's specific technical challenges (observable from job posts, blog, or product)
- Position contract-to-hire as risk reduction, not cost avoidance
- Never pitch "bodies" — pitch capability acceleration

---

## Persona 3: Engineering Manager / Technical Lead

### Role in Buying Process
**Influencer and evaluator.** Engineering managers own the day-to-day need — they know exactly what skills are missing and what the team needs. They typically initiate the request ("I need two more data engineers for Q2") and evaluate candidates technically, but do not control the vendor relationship or budget.

### Pain Points
- Team is stretched thin and burning out
- Roadmap commitments are at risk because of open headcount
- Internal recruiting sends candidates who cannot pass the technical bar
- Previous staffing vendors sent people who needed weeks of onboarding
- Spending too much of their own time sourcing and interviewing instead of managing

### Priorities
- Candidate fit: Will this person integrate into my team's codebase and workflows?
- Ramp speed: How quickly will the contractor be productive?
- Technical specificity: Does the vendor understand the difference between a data engineer who knows Spark and one who knows dbt?
- Low interview burden: Fewer interviews needed because pre-screening is rigorous

### Decision Criteria
1. Candidates who match the specific tech stack and seniority level
2. Evidence of technical vetting (not just resume screening)
3. Fast turnaround — every week without the hire costs sprint velocity
4. Contractor who can work within the team's existing processes (Agile, PR reviews, on-call if needed)

### How to Reach
- Email (responsive if the message references their specific team or open roles)
- LinkedIn (moderate response rate — they are on LinkedIn but not actively checking InMail)
- Internal champion: If the VP/CTO is already engaged, the Eng Manager is looped in for technical evaluation

### Messaging Notes
- Be specific about the role and technology ("I saw your team is hiring a Spark-focused data engineer")
- Offer to share candidate profiles rather than scheduling a sales call
- Emphasize pre-vetting and technical screening rigor
- Acknowledge that they have been burned by bad staffing experiences before

---

## Persona 4: Talent Acquisition / Recruiting Lead

### Role in Buying Process
**Gatekeeper or co-pilot.** In companies with a TA team, they manage the staffing vendor relationship, handle contracts, and coordinate interviews. They do not make the hiring decision but control access to the hiring manager and process flow.

### Pain Points
- Overwhelmed with open reqs, especially for hard-to-fill technical roles
- Measured on time-to-fill and cost-per-hire — both are suffering for specialized roles
- Existing staffing vendors submit too many unqualified candidates
- Engineering managers complain about candidate quality
- Managing multiple vendor relationships is administratively heavy

### Priorities
- Vendor reliability: Consistent candidate quality and responsiveness
- Process fit: Works within the existing ATS and interview workflow
- Administrative simplicity: Clean contracts, clear billing, no surprises
- Coverage for specific hard-to-fill roles that internal sourcing cannot crack

### Decision Criteria
1. Candidate quality (low rejection rate after submission)
2. Speed (first candidates within the SLA, typically 5-10 business days)
3. Competitive rates within the approved bill rate range
4. Willingness to work within the company's hiring process and tools

### How to Reach
- Email (professional, process-oriented messaging)
- LinkedIn (they actively use LinkedIn and are generally responsive)
- Job board interactions (if they post roles on staffing-adjacent platforms)

### Messaging Notes
- Speak their language: time-to-fill, submittal quality, fill rates
- Position CirrusLabs as a complement to internal recruiting, not a replacement
- Offer to fill specific roles they are struggling with, not a blanket staffing relationship
- Be willing to work within their process — do not insist on your own workflow

---

## Typical Buying Process

### Small Mid-Market (100-300 employees)
1. Eng Manager or CTO identifies need
2. CTO approves vendor engagement (often informal)
3. CirrusLabs submits candidates
4. Eng Manager interviews
5. CTO approves offer
6. **Timeline: 1-2 weeks from first contact to signed SOW**

### Larger Mid-Market (300-2000 employees)
1. Eng Manager identifies need and submits req
2. TA/Recruiting coordinates with approved vendor list
3. VP Eng approves new vendor if not on list
4. CirrusLabs submits candidates
5. Eng Manager + team interview
6. VP Eng or TA approves offer terms
7. **Timeline: 2-4 weeks from first contact to signed SOW**

### Key Insight
The fastest path to a staffing engagement is through the person who feels the pain most acutely (Eng Manager or VP Eng) combined with the person who can authorize the spend. Multi-threading — reaching both the budget holder and the hiring manager — dramatically increases conversion.

# Staffing Vertical — Observable Signals

## Signal Philosophy

Every staffing outreach email should reference something real and observable. "I noticed you're hiring" is the minimum bar — the best signals are specific, timely, and connected to a plausible staffing need.

Signals are ranked by strength (how likely they indicate a staffing need) and observability (how easily the signal can be detected via APIs and public data).

---

## Tier 1: Strong Signals (High Intent)

### 1. Active Job Posts for CirrusLabs Specialization Areas
**Signal:** Company has open requisitions for data engineers, ML engineers, cloud architects, or backend engineers.
**Why it matters:** Direct evidence of need for the exact roles CirrusLabs fills.
**Observable via:** Apollo job search, LinkedIn job posts, company careers page
**Freshness requirement:** Posted within the last 30 days
**Outreach angle:** "I saw you're looking for a [specific role]. We have pre-vetted candidates who can start within 2-3 weeks."

### 2. Multiple Open Reqs for the Same Role
**Signal:** Company has 3+ open positions for the same role type (e.g., 4 data engineer openings).
**Why it matters:** Multiple openings suggest internal recruiting cannot keep up with demand — classic staffing opportunity.
**Observable via:** Apollo organization job postings endpoint, LinkedIn job count
**Freshness requirement:** Multiple roles posted within 60 days
**Outreach angle:** "Hiring [number] [role] at once is tough through internal channels alone. We specialize in exactly this."

### 3. Stale Job Posts (Open 60+ Days)
**Signal:** Engineering role has been open for 2+ months without being filled.
**Why it matters:** Extended time-to-fill indicates the company is struggling to find qualified candidates — the core problem staffing solves.
**Observable via:** Job post date comparison (posted_at vs current date)
**Freshness requirement:** Role must still be active (not expired/closed)
**Outreach angle:** "I noticed your [role] has been open since [month]. That's a hard hire — we typically fill similar roles in 2-3 weeks."

### 4. Contractor/Temp Roles Posted
**Signal:** Company posts roles explicitly labeled as contract, temp, or freelance for engineering positions.
**Why it matters:** They are already open to non-FTE engagement models — lowest friction path to a staffing conversation.
**Observable via:** Job post filters for employment type (contract, temp)
**Freshness requirement:** Posted within last 45 days
**Outreach angle:** "I saw you're open to contract engineers for [role]. We have candidates on our bench ready to interview this week."

---

## Tier 2: Moderate Signals (Likely Intent)

### 5. Funding Round in Last 12 Months
**Signal:** Company announced Series B+ funding or raised $20M+.
**Why it matters:** Post-funding companies almost always scale engineering headcount. The board expects growth milestones, and hiring is the biggest bottleneck.
**Observable via:** Apollo organization enrichment, Crunchbase, news APIs
**Freshness requirement:** Funding within last 12 months (6 months is optimal)
**Outreach angle:** "Congrats on the [Series X]. Based on what we've seen with similar companies post-raise, scaling the data/ML team is usually the first bottleneck."

### 6. Hiring Velocity Spike
**Signal:** Company posted 5+ new engineering roles in the last 30 days (significantly above their normal rate).
**Why it matters:** A sudden increase in engineering hiring signals a project ramp, expansion, or strategic shift — all create staffing opportunities.
**Observable via:** Apollo job posting counts over time, LinkedIn hiring activity
**Freshness requirement:** Spike must be recent (last 30 days vs. prior 30 days)
**Outreach angle:** "I noticed [Company] has ramped up engineering hiring significantly this month. When teams scale fast, staffing augmentation can bridge the gap while your internal recruiting pipeline catches up."

### 7. Engineering Leadership Change
**Signal:** New VP of Engineering, CTO, or Head of Engineering joined within last 6 months.
**Why it matters:** New engineering leaders typically reshape teams, bring in trusted vendors, and accelerate hiring to establish their agenda.
**Observable via:** LinkedIn profile changes, Apollo people search with recent start dates
**Freshness requirement:** Leadership change within last 6 months
**Outreach angle:** "I saw you recently joined [Company] as [title]. New engineering leaders often need to move fast on hiring — we can help with [specific role types]."

### 8. Product Launch or Expansion Announcement
**Signal:** Company announces new product, new market entry, or major feature release.
**Why it matters:** Product expansion creates project-specific engineering demand that often exceeds existing team capacity.
**Observable via:** Press releases, company blog, news APIs, social media
**Freshness requirement:** Announcement within last 3 months
**Outreach angle:** "I read about [Company]'s [product/expansion]. Building that out usually means scaling the [data/backend/infra] team — that's exactly where we help."

---

## Tier 3: Weak Signals (Possible Intent)

### 9. Technology Migration or Platform Rebuild
**Signal:** Company mentions migrating to cloud, rebuilding data platform, or adopting new ML infrastructure.
**Why it matters:** Migrations require specialized skills for a bounded period — ideal for contract staffing.
**Observable via:** Engineering blog posts, conference talks, job post descriptions mentioning migration
**Freshness requirement:** Mentioned within last 6 months
**Outreach angle:** "[Technology] migrations need specialists who've done it before. We have engineers who've completed [similar migrations] and can start quickly."

### 10. Acquisition or Merger
**Signal:** Company acquired another company or was acquired.
**Why it matters:** Post-acquisition integration requires additional engineering capacity for system consolidation, data migration, and platform unification.
**Observable via:** News APIs, Apollo organization events, Crunchbase
**Freshness requirement:** Acquisition within last 6 months
**Outreach angle:** "Post-acquisition integrations always need more engineering hands than planned. We can provide [specific capability] engineers for the integration sprint."

### 11. Engineering Team Attrition
**Signal:** Multiple engineers leaving the company (visible via LinkedIn profile changes showing departures).
**Why it matters:** Attrition creates backfill urgency — the team is shrinking while the work stays the same.
**Observable via:** LinkedIn company page (departures), Glassdoor reviews mentioning turnover
**Freshness requirement:** Observable pattern in last 3 months
**Outreach angle:** Approach carefully — do not reference attrition directly. Instead, reference the open roles that result from it.

### 12. Conference Sponsorship or Hiring Events
**Signal:** Company sponsors data/ML/cloud conferences or hosts recruiting events.
**Why it matters:** Investment in hiring events indicates active need and budget allocation for talent acquisition.
**Observable via:** Conference sponsor lists, Eventbrite, Meetup.com
**Freshness requirement:** Event within last 3 months
**Outreach angle:** "I saw [Company] at [conference] — sounds like you're investing heavily in [area]. We specialize in placing engineers in exactly that space."

---

## Signal Combinations (Highest Priority)

The strongest outreach targets combine multiple signals. Prioritize leads that match two or more:

| Combination | Priority | Why |
|------------|----------|-----|
| Active job posts + stale roles (60+ days) | Highest | They need the role AND cannot fill it internally |
| Funding round + hiring velocity spike | Highest | Capital + action = immediate staffing need |
| Multiple open reqs + contractor roles posted | High | Volume need + openness to non-FTE |
| New eng leadership + hiring spike | High | New leader building their team fast |
| Product launch + active hiring | High | Project-driven urgency with real timeline |
| Funding + no active hiring yet | Moderate | Capital deployed but hiring not started — early outreach |

# Staffing — Lead Scoring Guide

> This file provides qualitative scoring philosophy and a suggested weighted model for evaluating staffing leads. These weights are starter guidance — adjust based on campaign results and vertical learnings.

---

## Scoring Philosophy

**Urgency > Fit.**

In staffing, the highest-value leads are companies with immediate, unfilled hiring needs. A Series B startup desperately trying to fill 5 data engineer roles is a better lead than a Fortune 500 with a vague "always hiring" posture — even if the Fortune 500 is a bigger logo.

Key principle: **Observable urgency signals matter more than company profile alone.** A company actively posting roles, losing engineers, or scaling after funding is far more likely to engage than one that merely fits the ICP on paper.

---

## Suggested Weighted Scoring Model (300-Point Scale)

> These weights align with the existing `scoring.ts` threshold of 170 points. Leads scoring 170+ should be enriched for contacts; below 170, skip enrichment to save API credits.

### Dimension 1: Hiring Signal Strength (0–100 points)

| Signal Factor | Points | Criteria |
|--------------|--------|----------|
| Active job postings for target roles | 0–40 | 40 = 5+ open roles matching our placement capabilities; 20 = 2–4 roles; 10 = 1 role; 0 = no matching roles |
| Role seniority alignment | 0–20 | 20 = senior/staff/lead roles (higher bill rates); 10 = mid-level; 5 = junior only |
| Posting freshness | 0–20 | 20 = posted within 7 days; 10 = 8–30 days; 5 = 31–60 days; 0 = 60+ days |
| Hiring velocity indicator | 0–20 | 20 = multiple roles posted in last 30 days (team build-out); 10 = steady hiring; 0 = single stale post |

### Dimension 2: Company Profile Fit (0–100 points)

| Profile Factor | Points | Criteria |
|---------------|--------|----------|
| Company size (employees) | 0–25 | 25 = 100–2,000 (sweet spot for staffing); 15 = 50–99 or 2,001–5,000; 10 = 5,001–10,000; 5 = <50 or >10,000 |
| Funding stage / financial health | 0–25 | 25 = Series A–C with recent round; 15 = Series D+ or profitable growth; 10 = bootstrapped profitable; 5 = pre-seed/seed |
| Industry alignment | 0–25 | 25 = tech, fintech, healthtech, e-commerce (high engineering density); 15 = SaaS, enterprise software; 10 = other tech-adjacent; 5 = non-tech |
| Geography | 0–25 | 25 = primary markets (US, Singapore); 15 = secondary markets (UK, Canada, Australia); 10 = EMEA; 5 = other |

### Dimension 3: Engagement Likelihood (0–100 points)

| Engagement Factor | Points | Criteria |
|------------------|--------|----------|
| Decision-maker accessibility | 0–30 | 30 = VP Eng/CTO with verified email; 20 = Eng Manager with email; 10 = HR/TA lead only; 0 = no contacts found |
| Prior agency usage signals | 0–25 | 25 = known to use staffing agencies (job posts mention agency, LinkedIn shows contract hires); 10 = unknown; 0 = explicitly anti-agency |
| Company hiring pain indicators | 0–25 | 25 = roles open 60+ days (struggling to fill); 15 = roles open 30–59 days; 5 = roles open <30 days; 0 = no pain signal |
| Competitor displacement opportunity | 0–20 | 20 = using generalist agency (opportunity to upgrade); 10 = no known agency; 0 = using specialized competitor |

---

## Scoring Tiers

| Tier | Score Range | Action | Rationale |
|------|------------|--------|-----------|
| 🔥 Hot | 220–300 | Enrich + prioritize outreach | Strong signals + great fit + high engagement likelihood |
| ✅ Strong | 170–219 | Enrich + standard outreach | Good signals or fit, worth pursuing |
| 🟡 Warm | 120–169 | Monitor, do not enrich yet | Some potential but missing key signals — revisit next quarter |
| ⬜ Low | < 120 | Skip | Poor fit or no urgency signals — not worth the API credits |

---

## Disqualification Criteria (Automatic Score = 0)

Skip these companies regardless of other signals:

- **No engineering roles posted** and no other hiring urgency signals
- **Company size < 20 employees** — unlikely to use external staffing for engineering
- **Government/public sector** without explicit contractor procurement process
- **Companies with active hiring freezes** (check recent news, layoff trackers)
- **Competitors** — other staffing/recruiting agencies
- **Companies outside serviceable geography** — no timezone overlap with CirrusLabs delivery

## Quick Reference: What Makes a Great Staffing Lead?

1. **Multiple open engineering roles** posted in the last 2 weeks (urgency)
2. **Series A–C company** or mid-market with 100–2,000 employees (budget + need)
3. **VP Engineering or CTO** with verified contact info (reachable decision-maker)
4. **Roles open for 30+ days** (pain — they're struggling to fill internally)
5. **Tech-forward industry** with high engineering density (fintech, e-commerce, SaaS)
6. **Located in US or Singapore** (CirrusLabs primary markets)
